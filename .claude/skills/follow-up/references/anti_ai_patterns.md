# Anti-AI Writing Patterns

Rules for writing that doesn't sound like it was generated. Apply to all outreach and follow-up emails.

---

## Hard rules (never break)

1. **No em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead.
2. **No rule-of-three lists.** Two items fine. Four fine. Three every time is an AI tell.
3. **No contrast framing.** "It's not X, it's Y" and all variants. Just say what you mean.
4. **No staccato bursts.** Three or more short sentences in a row for drama. Vary length naturally.
5. **No rhetorical transitions.** "The catch?" "But here's the thing." "So what does this mean?"
6. **No self-narration.** Don't announce your own points. "This highlights," "here's why this matters" = delete.
7. **No vague scale.** "Many," "significant," "strong results" = replace with a number or a concrete situation. If you don't have one, cut the claim.
8. **No false openers.** "Quick question," "Just wanted to check in," "Circling back," "Touching base," "Wanted to loop back" = cut. If you need a sentence to justify sending the email, the email isn't ready.
9. **No -ing phrases tacked to sentence ends.** "You appear in two queries, highlighting the importance of AI visibility." Cut everything after the comma. If the point matters, make it in the next sentence with specifics.
10. **No significance inflation.** "Marking a pivotal moment," "setting the stage for," "a testament to" = if something matters, say why with a number or fact.
11. **No "serves as / stands as / functions as."** Replace with "is."
12. **No formal transition openers.** "However," "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally" at sentence start = delete or rewrite.

---

## Banned words

**Adjectives:** cutting-edge, innovative, comprehensive, seamless, robust, game-changing, transformative, holistic, dynamic, compelling

**Verbs:** leverage, utilize, optimize, empower, elevate, harness, streamline, unlock, navigate (figurative), facilitate

**Phrases:** "in today's landscape", "at its core", "it's worth noting", "here's the thing", "the reality is", "level up", "move the needle", "bridges the gap"

**Plain replacements:**

| Instead of | Write |
|---|---|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help |
| optimize | improve |
| leverage | use |
| implement | build, start |
| navigate | handle |
| streamline | simplify |
| augment | add to |
| cultivate | build, grow |
| execute | do |

## Structural patterns

**One idea, one paragraph.** Even if it's one sentence. Rhythm comes from variation between short and long paragraphs, not from uniformity.

**Conclusion first.** State what you found or why you're writing in the first sentence. Then earn it. Never build up to the point.

**Specificity over credibility language.** Not "many customers" but "27 customers." Not "significant drop" but "conversion dropped 40%." Named brands, not "several enterprise clients." If you have a number, use it. If you don't, describe the concrete situation instead.

**Name things.** Tefal and De'Longhi, not "several live clients." Shopify Plus, not "major ecommerce platforms." Specific query examples, not "various search terms."

**First person, active.** "I ran," "I built," "we put." Never passive constructions ("an audit was conducted"), never abstract ("the analysis revealed").

**Show reasoning when relevant.** Not "X is important" but why you think so. Not "this is a growing trend" but what you saw that made you think so.

---

## Tone guardrails

**Professional but not cold.** Starting sentences with "And," "But," "So" is fine and often better than formal connectors.

**Admitting uncertainty is strength.** "Not sure if this is relevant" reads more honest than false confidence. But don't overdo it — one uncertainty signal per email max.

**No performative casualness.** Don't force informal openers to sound "authentic." "This stat surprised me" or "Something I noticed" in a cold email reads as a performance of authenticity, which is the opposite of authentic. Just say the thing.

**No buildup, no reveal.** Don't write "Here's the thing:" or "What I found was surprising:" Just say what you found.

**Permitted:**
- Starting sentences with "And," "But," "So," "Or"
- Sentence fragments when they serve rhythm
- Questions, but only genuine ones (not rhetorical)

## Self-check (run after writing)

1. Any em dashes? Replace with comma or period.
2. Any group of exactly three? Change to two or four.
3. Any banned word? Swap for plain version.
4. Any sentence that announces the next sentence? Delete it.
5. Any vague scale claim? Replace with a number or cut it.
6. Any -ing phrase stapled to sentence end? Cut it.
7. Conclusion in the first sentence? If not, move it there.
8. Any performative casualness? ("Something I noticed," "This surprised me") — cut it, just say the thing.
9. Read it out loud. Does it sound like a person or a brochure?

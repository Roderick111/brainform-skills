# Follow-Up Principles

## What a follow-up is

A reason to reply. Not a reminder that you exist.

## Before writing, answer honestly

1. What did I ask them to do?
2. Why haven't they done it? (busy, unclear value, ask too big, wrong person, forgot)
3. **What new thing does this email give them?** If the answer is "nothing, just reminding them," don't send it. Find something new or don't write.
4. What's the smallest thing I can ask that moves this forward?

## Core rules

**Natural and short.** Write like a person texting a colleague, not a sales rep following a playbook. If it sounds like a template, rewrite.

**Give, don't diagnose.** Every follow-up must deliver something valuable: a product benefit, a useful stat, a concrete picture of what changes, an audit, context on what others in their space are doing. If the email only points out a problem and asks for time, it's extracting, not giving. The recipient should think "this person gave me something," not "this person found my flaw."

**No outside diagnosis.** You're a stranger. You haven't earned the right to tell them what's wrong with their site, their strategy, or their visibility. Customer empathy is fine ("a shopper trying to build a skincare routine has to read every page"). Telling the brand they're failing is not ("your site doesn't help customers choose," "you don't show up in ChatGPT"). Let the audit or demo reveal gaps. The email delivers value.

**Add a new type of value every time.** Never re-send the same link, re-state the same observation, or repeat the same ask. Each email must use a different value type from all previous emails in the thread: product benefit, market stat, concrete picture, audit/demo, social proof. Re-sending the same audit link with a different problem sentence is not a follow-up, it's the same email with new paint.

**One email, one job.** Don't deliver a document, ask a question, and propose next steps in one message. Pick one.

**1 email = consumer job → business job → personal job.** Every B2B purchase decision is driven by a chain. The product solves the consumer job (shopper gets guidance). That produces a business outcome (conversion, fewer returns, fewer tickets). That business outcome serves the stakeholder's personal job (recognition, career safety, winning pitches, financial security, conference talks).

The personal job is the destination, not a filter. The business job is the vehicle, not the destination. 90% of a decision maker's motivation is personal and emotional. A product that improves the business metric but threatens the stakeholder's personal standing will not get bought. A product that marginally improves the metric but makes the stakeholder look like a visionary will.

**The three-layer pattern in the email:** all three layers should be implicit. The reader never sees the words "personal job." They feel it through how the business outcome is framed.

- "Your team stops absorbing a problem they didn't create" — Head of Support sees: this proves tickets are a site problem, not mine
- "Whoever reports on this first owns the narrative" — Head of eCommerce sees: this is my QBR win
- "A few brands your size are doing this" + live names — CMO sees: I could be the one who brought this in
- "Next time a client asks what your AI story is" — Agency Founder sees: I win the next pitch

**Don't poke fears, reduce them.** Fear of blame is as powerful as desire for recognition. An employee can block a great solution because their boss might blame them if it breaks. Reduce fear by giving proof: live brand names (Tefal, De'Longhi), deployment speed (4 weeks), zero data prep. "Live with Tefal and De'Longhi" reduces the fear of recommending something unproven — the personal job of "not getting blamed" is served by the social proof, not by addressing the fear directly. Never use fear as pressure ("are your competitors doing X?").

Read `.claude/skills/outreach/references/role_briefings.md` for specific personal jobs per role. Read `context/jtbd_segments_full.md` for the full framework.

**Reference their world, not your previous email.** Don't summarize what you sent. They can scroll down. Enter with new value that connects to their job.

**Don't re-pitch.** If the original email explained Brainform, the follow-up does not. Repeating the value prop signals insecurity.

**Lower the barrier if the original ask was too big.** No reply to "20 min chat" → try a question they can answer in one line. No reply to "review these docs" → ask one specific question.

**No tease language.** "Something interesting came up" is manipulative filler. Either say what it is or ask a direct question. Don't dangle vague curiosity bait.

**No apologies for writing again.** "Sorry to bother", "I know you're busy", "just one more" — all weak. If the email is worth sending, send it without apologizing.

**Autonomy, not pressure.** "Worth a look if relevant" > "when can we chat." "If that's on your roadmap" > "you're missing out." Let the recipient decide if this applies to them. No manufactured urgency, no fear-poking, no implying they're behind.

## Structure

2nd touch: under 60 words. New angle, can include a link (different from original).
3rd touch: under 40 words. Very direct. One question or one fact.
4th touch: breakup. Redirect question + clean close.

Three parts max:
1. **The move** (1-2 lines): the reason this email exists. New info, new question, or new angle.
2. **Soft CTA** (1 line): always include. Even tiny ("on your radar?", "relevant for you?").

No context anchors like "following up on" or "as I mentioned." Just start.

## Tone

Match the tone of the relationship. Not arrogant, not apologetic. Direct and human. The frame is: I have something useful, here it is, interested or not?

## Psychology

- Every recipient asks: is this person here to extract from me or give to me?
- Specificity is the signal. Generic follow-ups get ignored because they could have been sent to anyone.
- Autonomy preservation: "if relevant" and "on your radar" > "when can we schedule"
- The biggest competitor is always the status quo.

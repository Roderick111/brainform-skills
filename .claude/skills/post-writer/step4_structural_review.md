# Step 4: Structural Review

Review the draft against content principles. This is not about grammar or AI patterns (that's Step 5). This is about whether the post is worth reading.

Go through each principle. For each one, quote the specific line(s) that violate it and explain what's wrong. If the post passes, say "pass" and move on.

After the review, output a revised version with all fixes applied.

---

## Principles

### 1. Share experience, not advice

The post should read like a dispatch from someone doing the work, not a lecture from someone teaching it. Don't tell the reader what they should do. Share what YOU did, what you saw, what went wrong, what surprised you, what you decided and why.

**Teachy/arrogant:** "Most ecom teams need to start tracking AI visibility."
**Dispatch:** "We started tracking AI visibility three months ago. What we found was weird."

If a sentence starts with "you should," "you need to," "most companies need to," or "it's important to" — rewrite it as a personal experience or observation. The reader extracts the advice themselves.

Vulnerability is an asset. Admitting what you didn't know, what took longer than expected, what you got wrong — this is what makes a post feel real. Polished authority is less interesting than honest progress.

### 2. Concrete over abstract

Every claim needs a specific anchor. Names, numbers, products, dates, situations.

**Abstract:** "AI agents are becoming a new discovery channel."
**Concrete:** "57% of shoppers used AI to find products in the past 9 months (CapGemini)."

**Abstract:** "We found that many stores aren't ready."
**Concrete:** "8 out of 10 stores we audited scored below 3 out of 10."

If a sentence makes a claim without a name, number, or specific example — flag it. Either add the specific or cut the claim.

### 3. Value density

Every sentence must carry information, an argument, or a story beat. If you can remove a sentence and nothing is lost, remove it.

Check for:
- **Setup sentences** that announce what comes next ("Let me explain why this matters"). Cut them, just say the thing.
- **Summary sentences** that repeat what was just said. Cut them.
- **Transition sentences** that exist only to connect paragraphs. Cut them, use white space instead.
- **Feature lists** disguised as value. "It checks 17 signals" tells the reader nothing. What do those signals reveal? What did you learn from them?

The goal: if the post is 10 sentences, each one should make the reader think or feel something new.

### 4. Value ladder awareness

Not a hard rule, but a useful lens. Where does this post sit?

- **Level 1:** Sharing a link or summarizing something. Low value, anyone can do this.
- **Level 2:** Personal interpretation, connecting dots across domains. Better.
- **Level 3:** Practical insight from your own work. What to do, based on what you actually did. Good.
- **Level 4:** Hidden strategic opportunity from proprietary data or experience that nobody else has. Best.

Most posts should aim for Level 3 minimum. Level 4 is when you're interpreting your own data, your own audit results, your own sales patterns. If you have proprietary findings, lead with them.

If the post is stuck at Level 1-2, ask: what do you know from direct experience that nobody else is saying?

### 5. Hook earns the post

Re-read the first 1-2 sentences in isolation. Imagine seeing them while scrolling LinkedIn at speed.

- Would you stop?
- Is there an information gap (you know enough to be curious, not enough to scroll past)?
- Is it dramatic, unresolved, or unexpected?
- Does it promise something the post actually delivers?

A hook that's interesting but disconnected from the insight is clickbait. A hook that's honest but boring is invisible. It needs to be both true and magnetic.

### 6. No jargon trap

Are you writing for your audience or for your peers?

The "nutritionist writes about microbiota while the client googles gluten-free bread" problem. If the post uses terminology your target reader wouldn't use in conversation, rewrite it in their language.

Exception: if jargon IS the point (introducing a concept), define it immediately with a concrete example.

### 7. Rhythm and white space

Read the post visually, not just for content.

- Do paragraph lengths vary? (Short, medium, short, medium — never three long blocks in a row)
- Is there breathing room between ideas?
- Does the eye move naturally down the post?

Dense walls of text kill LinkedIn posts. Even great content gets scrolled past if it looks heavy.

### 8. Ending

The post should end on one of:
- Something in progress or forward-looking ("We're running more of these. Patterns are getting clearer.")
- A clean CTA (link, question, invitation)
- The insight itself, if it's strong enough to be the last word

Never end with:
- A moral ("And that's why you should...")
- A summary of what you just said
- A generic motivational line
